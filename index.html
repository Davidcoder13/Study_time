Geologic Eras — In Order
Precambrian (~4.6 billion – 541 mya)

Formation of Earth; first single-celled life; no hard parts (poor fossil record)

Paleozoic (541 – 252 mya)

"Ancient life" — fish, amphibians, reptiles; first land plants; Pangaea forms; ends in mass extinction

Mesozoic (252 – 66 mya)

Age of Dinosaurs; first birds & mammals; flowering plants; Pangaea breaks up; K-Pg extinction

Cenozoic (66 mya – present)

Age of Mammals; humans evolve; Ice Ages; modern continents form

Age of the Earth
  • Earth is approximately 4.6 billion years old
  • Determined through radiometric (radioactive) dating of rocks and meteorites
  • Oldest known rocks ~4.0 billion years old
  • Moon rocks and meteorites confirm 4.6 billion year age
  • Half-life: time for half of radioactive atoms to decay (e.g., Carbon-14, Uranium-238)
Plate Tectonics — What It Is + Evidence

Earth's lithosphere is divided into large tectonic plates that move slowly (2–15 cm/year) due to convection currents in the mantle.

FOSSIL EVIDENCE

Same fossils (Mesosaurus, Glossopteris) found on continents now separated by oceans

CONTINENTAL FIT

Coastlines of South America and Africa fit together like puzzle pieces

ROCK MATCHING

Identical rock types and mountain ranges found on opposite sides of oceans (Appalachians = Scottish Highlands)

SEAFLOOR SPREADING

New crust forms at mid-ocean ridges; magnetic striping symmetrical on either side confirms movement

CLIMATE CLUES

Coal (tropical plant fossils) found in Antarctica; glacial deposits in Africa suggest ancient polar positions

Q: What causes tectonic plates to move?
Convection currents in the mantle — hot material rises, spreads, cools, and sinks, dragging the plates above it along.
Q: List the layers of the Earth from outermost to innermost and key properties of each.
Crust (thin, solid, rocky) → Mantle (thick, semi-solid, convection currents) → Outer Core (liquid iron-nickel, generates magnetic field) → Inner Core (solid iron-nickel, extremely hot ~5,400°C and under enormous pressure).

Rock Cycle & Rock Types
// igneous · sedimentary · metamorphic · definitions
Igneous Rocks
  • Formed from: cooling and solidification of magma/lava
  • Intrusive (plutonic): cools slowly underground → large crystals (e.g. granite, gabbro)
  • Extrusive (volcanic): cools quickly at surface → small/no crystals (e.g. basalt, obsidian, pumice)
  • Very hard; no layers; may have vesicles (holes) if cooled fast
Sedimentary Rocks
  • Formed from: compaction and cementation of sediment layers
  • Examples: sandstone, limestone, shale, conglomerate, coal
  • Key features: layers (strata), fossils, rounded grains
  • Most common on Earth's surface; found near ancient water bodies
  • Clastic (particles), chemical (precipitation), organic (remains)

Sponsored

Metamorphic Rocks
  • Formed from: heat and/or pressure transforming existing rock
  • Examples: marble (from limestone), quartzite (from sandstone), slate & schist (from shale), gneiss (from granite)
  • Key features: foliation (banding), crystals, wavy patterns
  • No fossils; found near tectonic boundaries & mountain roots
Key Rock Cycle Definitions
  • Magma: molten rock below Earth's surface
  • Lava: magma that has reached the surface
  • Weathering: breaking down rock in place (physical or chemical)
  • Erosion: transport of weathered material by water, wind, ice
  • Deposition: settling of eroded material
  • Lithification: compaction + cementation → sedimentary rock
  • Crystallization: magma cools → igneous rock
Southern Ontario Bedrock — Daily Impact

Southern Ontario is underlain mainly by limestone and shale (sedimentary rocks) from ancient shallow seas.

Hard Water

Limestone dissolves in groundwater → calcium/magnesium → scale in pipes & kettles

Building Material

Limestone quarried for construction (buildings, roads, aggregate)

Flat Landscape

Sedimentary rock creates relatively flat terrain → agriculture & urban development

Caves & Sinkholes

Dissolved limestone (karst topography) can create underground caves

Q: A rock with visible bands/foliation and no fossils. What type is it? How did it form?
It is a metamorphic rock. Foliation forms when minerals are squeezed and aligned under intense heat and pressure, without the rock melting completely. Examples include schist and gneiss. Fossils are destroyed in the process.

Q: Why does the Canadian Shield have such thin, poor soils?
Glaciers scraped the ancient bedrock, removing weathered material. The ice deposited much of it elsewhere. The hard igneous and metamorphic bedrock weathers very slowly, and the cold climate limits organic matter buildup, leaving only thin soil layers.

Climate Regions
// 6 factors · precipitation types · continental vs maritime
6 Factors That Affect Climate
① LATITUDE

Areas near the equator receive more direct solar energy → warmer. Poles receive less direct sunlight → colder. Closer to equator = warmer climate.

② OCEAN CURRENTS

Warm currents (e.g. Gulf Stream) warm nearby land. Cold currents cool land. Coastal areas are moderated.

③ ALTITUDE / ELEVATION

Higher elevation → thinner air → less heat retained → cooler temperatures (~6.5°C drop per 1000m gained).

④ LARGE BODIES OF WATER

Water heats and cools slowly (maritime effect) → moderates temperature extremes for nearby land. Interior areas have more extreme temperatures.

⑤ PREVAILING WINDS

Winds carry moisture and temperature from their source. Westerlies bring Pacific air to BC. Wind direction determines what kind of air mass arrives.

⑥ RELIEF (MOUNTAINS)

Mountains force air to rise → cool → precipitation on windward side (orographic). Leeward side is dry (rain shadow effect).

Vancouver — Top 3 Climate Factors
  • ① Ocean Currents: Warm North Pacific Current moderates temperatures (mild, wet winters)
  • ② Prevailing Winds: Westerlies bring moist Pacific air onshore → high precipitation
  • ③ Relief (Mountains): Coast Mountains force moist air up → orographic precipitation; very high rainfall on windward slopes
Toronto — Top 3 Climate Factors
  • ① Latitude: Mid-latitude location → cold winters, warm summers (4 distinct seasons)
  • ② Large Bodies of Water: Lake Ontario moderates temperature (lake-effect snow in winter; cooler summers near shore)
  • ③ Prevailing Winds: Variable winds bring cold Arctic air in winter and warm Gulf air in summer → temperature extremes
Continental vs Maritime Climate
Continental
  • →Temperature: Extreme — very hot summers, very cold winters (large annual range)
  • →Precipitation: Low and seasonal; mostly summer convectional rain; dry winters
Maritime
  • →Temperature: Moderate — mild summers, mild winters (small annual range)
  • →Precipitation: High year-round; frontal & orographic rain; wet winters
3 Types of Precipitation
  • ① Convectional: Sun heats ground → warm moist air rises → cools → condenses → rain. Mostly in summer. Often thunderstorms.
  • ② Orographic (Relief): Air forced over mountains → rises & cools → precipitation on windward side. Rain shadow on leeward.
  • ③ Frontal (Cyclonic): Warm & cold air masses meet → warm air rises over cold → precipitation along frontal boundary. Common in fall/winter.
Reading Climate Graphs
  • Continental graph: Large temperature range (30°C+), low & seasonal precip, summer peak for rain
  • Maritime graph: Small temperature range (<15°C), high year-round precip, winter precipitation peak
  • Look for: How much do the temperatures fluctuate? Is precipitation evenly spread or seasonal?
Q: Which type of precipitation occurs because of mountains? Explain the process.
Orographic (Relief) precipitation. Moist air is forced upward as it meets a mountain range. As the air rises, it cools, reaches its dew point, and moisture condenses into clouds and precipitation on the windward (facing the wind) side. On the leeward (opposite) side, dry air descends and warms, creating a rain shadow — an area of low precipitation.

Soil & Vegetation
// soil profile · permafrost · vegetation zones · treelines
4 Components of Soil
  • Mineral particles — weathered rock (sand, silt, clay)
  • Organic matter (humus) — decomposed plant/animal material; dark; nutrient-rich
  • Water — held in pore spaces; essential for plant growth
  • Air — in pore spaces; needed by roots and soil organisms
  • Loam = ideal mix of sand, silt, and clay
Soil Profile — Horizons
  • O Horizon: organic layer — leaf litter, partially decomposed material on surface
  • A Horizon (topsoil): humus-rich; dark; where most plant roots are; most fertile
  • B Horizon (subsoil): minerals leached from above accumulate here; less organic matter
  • C Horizon: weathered parent rock
  • R Horizon: solid bedrock
Key Soil Definitions
  • Humus: dark, nutrient-rich organic matter from decomposed organisms
  • Leaching: water moving through soil carries nutrients downward, away from roots
  • Loam: ideal soil — balanced mix of sand, silt, clay; retains moisture & drains well
  • Permafrost: ground that remains frozen for 2+ years; found in northern Canada (Yukon, NWT, Nunavut)
  • Active Layer: top layer of permafrost that thaws in summer; above the permanently frozen ground
Vegetation Zones — Key Facts
  • Southern Ontario is in the Mixed Forest (Great Lakes-St. Lawrence Forest) zone
  • Natural vegetation here has largely been cleared for agriculture and urban development — most forest is gone
  • Boreal Shield / Taiga is a transition zone between boreal forest and tundra — trees become sparse and shorter toward the north
  • Coniferous trees (needles, cone-bearing, evergreen) found in boreal/northern Canada
  • Deciduous trees (broad leaves, lose leaves in fall) found in southern Ontario & eastern Canada
Coniferous vs Deciduous Trees
Coniferous
  • →Needle-shaped leaves
  • →Stay green year-round (evergreen)
  • →Produce cones (pine, spruce, fir)
Deciduous
  • →Broad, flat leaves
  • →Drop leaves in autumn
  • →Produce flowers/fruit (maple, oak, beech)
Treelines — 3 Types & Causes
  • Arctic Treeline: Where trees can no longer survive due to cold temperatures — marks the northern limit of forests in Canada
  • Alpine Treeline: On mountains — where altitude makes it too cold for trees to grow (above this = alpine meadow/rock)
  • 3 Factors causing treelines: (1) Too cold / short growing season, (2) Permafrost prevents root growth, (3) Strong winds damage or stunt tree growth

Natural Disasters
// emergency response kit · preparedness
Emergency Response Kit — What to Include
💧 Water

2 litres per person per day; at least 72-hour supply (3 days minimum)

🥫 Food

Non-perishable food (canned goods, dried food); manual can opener; 72-hour supply

🔦 Flashlight & Radio

Battery-powered or hand-crank flashlight; battery/hand-crank radio for emergency alerts; extra batteries

🩹 First Aid Kit

Bandages, antiseptic, medications, first aid manual; any prescription medications for family members

🧥 Warmth & Shelter

Warm clothes, sturdy shoes, blankets/sleeping bags; extra clothing for weather changes

📋 Documents & Cash

Copies of important documents (ID, insurance); cash (ATMs may not work); emergency contact list

🔧 Tools

Whistle (signal for help), multi-tool/knife, matches/lighter in waterproof container, duct tape

🐾 Special Needs

Baby supplies, pet food, hearing aid batteries, glasses — account for all family members' specific needs

Q: Why do emergency kits recommend a 72-hour (3-day) supply of food and water?
It typically takes emergency services at least 72 hours to reach and assist all affected residents after a major disaster. During this period, utilities (water, electricity), roads, and stores may be unavailable. A 72-hour supply allows a household to be self-sufficient until official help arrives.
