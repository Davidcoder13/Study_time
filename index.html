Demography & the Census
// what is demography · why it matters · census basics
What Is Demography?

Demography is the scientific study of human populations — including their size, structure, distribution, and how they change over time through births, deaths, and migration.


Why Is It Important?

  • Helps governments plan services — schools, hospitals, roads, housing
  • Predicts future labour market needs and shortages
  • Informs immigration policy (how many people Canada needs)
  • Helps understand aging populations and pension pressures
  • Used by businesses to target markets and plan locations
  • Allows comparison of well-being between countries (HDI, poverty)
What Is a Census?
  • An official count of every person living in a country at a specific point in time
  • Canada conducts a census every 5 years (Statistics Canada)
  • Collects data on: age, gender, language, income, education, housing, ethnicity, immigration status
  • Every household is legally required to respond
  • Canada's census is one of the most detailed in the world

Why Is It Important?

  • Governments use it to allocate funding (more people = more funding for that region)
  • Determines how many seats each province gets in Parliament
  • Helps track trends over time — aging, urbanization, diversity
  • Essential for planning schools, transit, healthcare facilities
Q: Why is demography important for Canada's government?
Demography helps the government understand who lives in Canada now and predict what the population will look like in the future. This is critical for planning — building enough schools for children, hospitals for seniors, allocating funding fairly, and deciding how many immigrants Canada needs to sustain its workforce and economy as the population ages.
Q: What is a census, and why does Canada conduct one every 5 years?
A census is an official count and survey of every person in the country at a single point in time. Canada conducts one every 5 years because populations change — people are born, die, move, and immigrate — and governments need current, accurate data to make good decisions about funding, services, and policy. Without the census, governments would have to guess how many people need roads, hospitals, and schools.

What the Pyramid Shape Tells You
  • Wide base (0–14): high birth rate → fast-growing, young population
  • Narrow top (65+): high death rate → short life expectancy
  • Even/wide top: low death rate → long life expectancy, aging population
  • Narrow middle: past wars or emigration reduced that generation
  • Bulge in working age (20–40): large immigration wave into that country
  • Gender imbalance: wars kill more males → bars unequal on left vs right
  • Future growth: wide base = population will grow; narrow base = will shrink
Dependency Load (Dependency Ratio)

The dependency load is the proportion of the population that is not working (dependents) compared to those who are working (supporters).


  • Dependents = children (0–14) + seniors (65+)
  • Workers = people aged 15–64
  • High dependency = more strain on working population (more taxes needed)
  • Canada's aging baby boomers are increasing the dependency load
  • More seniors per worker → pressure on pensions, healthcare, social programs
  • Immigration helps by adding working-age people to Canada's workforce
Baby Boom & Baby Boom Echo

BABY BOOM (1946–1965)

  • Surge of births after World War II — soldiers returned home, families reunited
  • Lasted ~20 years; unusually high birth rates across North America
  • Boomers now aged ~60–79 — aging out of workforce, retiring
  • Shows up as a wide bulge in the middle of Canada's pyramid
  • Creating pressure on healthcare, CPP, and OAS pension systems

BABY BOOM ECHO (1980s–1995)

  • The children of Baby Boomers — a second, smaller surge in births
  • Also called "Echo Boomers" or Millennials
  • Visible as a secondary bulge slightly below the boom bulge
  • Smaller than the original boom because each Boomer had fewer kids
  • Now aged ~30–45 — the core of today's workforce
Q: A pyramid has a very wide base and a narrow top. What type of pyramid is this, and what does it tell you about the country?
This is an Expansive pyramid. The wide base means a high birth rate — many children are being born. The narrow top means a high death rate and low life expectancy — few people survive to old age. The population is young and growing rapidly. This is typical of Less Economically Developed Countries (LEDCs) where healthcare is limited and families have many children.
Q: What is the dependency load, and why is it increasing in Canada?
The dependency load is the ratio of non-working people (children 0–14 and seniors 65+) to working-age people (15–64). It is increasing in Canada because the Baby Boomers (born 1946–1965) are now retiring, creating a large wave of seniors who no longer work and who rely on pensions and healthcare. At the same time, Canada's birth rate is low (below replacement level), so there are fewer young people entering the workforce to support them. This means fewer workers are paying taxes to fund more services for more dependents.

Natural Increase
// trends in Canada · fertility rate · baby boom impacts · aging population
Natural Increase — Canadian Trends
  • Natural Increase = Birth Rate − Death Rate
  • Canada's natural increase has been declining for decades
  • Birth rate has fallen steadily since the post-WWII baby boom
  • Canada's current fertility rate (~1.4) is below replacement level (2.1)
  • This means Canada cannot grow naturally — deaths will eventually exceed births without immigration
  • Canada relies heavily on immigration to maintain population growth
Fertility Rate — Definition & Trend

The fertility rate (TFR) is the average number of children a woman is expected to have over her lifetime.


  • Replacement rate = 2.1 children per woman (needed to keep population stable)
  • Canada's current TFR ≈ 1.4 — well below replacement
  • Why is it declining?
  • Women pursuing higher education and careers → having children later (or not at all)
  • High cost of living, housing, and childcare
  • Shift from rural (children = labour) to urban (children = expense) lifestyle
  • Greater access to contraception and family planning
Impacts of the Baby Boom & Aging Population
  • Baby Boomers (born 1946–1965) are now retiring in large waves
  • More retirees drawing on Canada Pension Plan (CPP) and healthcare
  • Fewer workers paying into social programs → financial strain
  • Demand for seniors services — long-term care homes, hospitals — increasing rapidly
  • Many skilled Boomers leaving the workforce creates labour shortages (e.g., nursing, trades)
  • Government increasing immigration targets to fill workforce gaps
  • Growing strain on younger generations (Echo Boomers / Millennials) who must support more retirees
Why Canada Needs Immigration
  • Natural increase alone is insufficient to grow the population
  • Without immigration, workforce would shrink as boomers retire
  • Immigration brings younger, working-age people who pay taxes
  • Helps fill skilled labour gaps in healthcare, tech, trades
  • Contributes to economic growth — immigrants start businesses, spend money
  • Canada's immigration target: ~500,000 per year by 2025
Q: What is the fertility rate, and what is the trend in Canada? Why does this matter?
The fertility rate (TFR) is the average number of children a woman will have in her lifetime. Canada's current TFR is approximately 1.4, which is well below the replacement rate of 2.1. This means that without immigration, Canada's population would eventually start declining. The trend is driven by women pursuing education and careers, the high cost of raising children in cities, and shifting cultural values. This matters because a shrinking population means fewer workers, less tax revenue, and more pressure on social programs like healthcare and pensions.

Canada's Indigenous Population
// First Nations · Métis · Inuit · key facts
First Nations
  • Largest Indigenous group in Canada
  • Over 630 recognized First Nations communities
  • Live on-reserve and off-reserve (cities)
  • Diverse — hundreds of distinct nations, languages, cultures
  • Governed by the Indian Act (a federal law, often criticized)
  • Examples: Ojibwe, Cree, Mohawk, Mi'kmaq, Haida
Métis
  • People of mixed Indigenous and European ancestry
  • Emerged through relationships between First Nations women and European (mainly French) fur traders
  • Distinct culture, language (Michif), and traditions
  • Strong historical presence in western Canada (Manitoba, Saskatchewan)
  • Led by Louis Riel — key figure in Métis resistance (Red River and Northwest Resistances)
Inuit
  • Indigenous peoples of Arctic Canada (Nunavut, NWT, northern Québec, Labrador)
  • Smallest of the three groups
  • Adapted to extreme northern environments
  • Traditional practices: hunting (seals, whales, caribou), fishing, dog sledding
  • Language: Inuktitut
  • Nunavut (created 1999) is a self-governing Inuit territory
Key Population Facts
  • Indigenous peoples make up about 5% of Canada's population
  • One of the fastest-growing populations in Canada
  • Young population — median age is lower than the Canadian average
  • About 60% of First Nations members live off-reserve, many in cities
  • Winnipeg and Vancouver have some of the largest urban Indigenous populations
  • Indigenous population growing rapidly due to higher birth rates and improved census identification
Context & Challenges
  • Legacy of colonialism, residential schools, and loss of land
  • Higher rates of poverty, housing insecurity, and health issues
  • Ongoing land claims and treaty negotiations
  • Calls to Action from Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC)
  • Growing political representation and self-governance movements
  • Important role in Canadian identity, culture, and environmental stewardship
Q: Name the three groups of Indigenous peoples in Canada and briefly describe each.
First Nations: The largest Indigenous group with over 630 communities and hundreds of distinct languages and cultures. They live both on-reserve and in cities across Canada.

Métis: People of mixed Indigenous and European ancestry, emerging from relationships between First Nations women and European (mostly French) fur traders. They have their own distinct culture and language (Michif), with roots especially in western Canada.

Inuit: Indigenous peoples of the Arctic North (Nunavut, northern Quebec, Labrador, NWT). They are adapted to extreme northern environments, speak Inuktitut, and have their own territory of Nunavut (1999).

Migration
// immigration vs emigration · push/pull · 3 classes · points system
Immigration vs. Emigration
  • Immigration: moving into a country permanently — Canada is the destination
  • Emigration: moving out of a country permanently — Canada is the origin
  • Immigrant: a person who arrives in a new country to live
  • Emigrant: a person who has left their home country
  • Net Migration: Immigration − Emigration = the net gain or loss of people
  • Canada gains far more immigrants than it loses emigrants → positive net migration
Immigration Trends in Canada
  • Canada accepts ~400,000–500,000 immigrants per year (one of the highest rates per capita in the world)
  • Before 1967: most immigrants came from Europe (UK, Italy, Germany, Ukraine)
  • After 1967 points system: opened to all countries equally → shift toward Asia, Caribbean, Africa
  • Top source countries today: India, China, Philippines, Nigeria, Pakistan
  • Immigration is Canada's primary source of population growth (natural increase is too low)
  • Targets increasing to fill aging workforce gaps
Push Factors

Negative conditions that drive people to leave their home country:

  • War, armed conflict, and political instability
  • Political persecution or lack of freedom
  • Extreme poverty and unemployment
  • Famine, drought, or natural disasters
  • Lack of healthcare or educational opportunities
  • Religious or ethnic persecution
  • Environmental degradation (pollution, climate change)
Pull Factors

Positive conditions that attract people to Canada:

  • Job opportunities and strong economy
  • Political stability and freedom
  • High standard of living
  • Universal healthcare and social programs
  • Excellent education system
  • Family members already living in Canada
  • Canada's reputation as multicultural and welcoming
  • Safe environment, rule of law
Canada's 3 Immigration Classes
① ECONOMIC CLASS (~58%)

Selected using the points system. Immigrants chosen for their ability to work and contribute economically. Includes Skilled Workers, Trades Workers, and Business Immigrants.

② FAMILY CLASS (~27%)

Sponsored by a Canadian citizen or permanent resident. Includes spouses, common-law partners, dependent children, parents, and grandparents. The sponsor must financially support the newcomer.

③ REFUGEE CLASS (~15%)

People fleeing war, persecution, or danger. Includes Government-Assisted Refugees (GARs), Privately Sponsored Refugees, and Asylum Seekers who claim protection after arriving in Canada.

The Points System — Economic Immigration
Applicants are scored out of 100 points across 6 categories. A minimum of 67 points is typically required to qualify. The system selects immigrants most likely to succeed economically in Canada.
Factor Max Points Why Canada Values It
① Education 25 pts Higher education = more skills. PhD/Master's = 25 pts. Bachelor's ≈ 20 pts. More educated → more productive worker.
② Language Skills 28 pts English and/or French fluency. Tested via IELTS or TEF. Better language = better integration, employment, and communication.
③ Work Experience 15 pts Skilled work experience in the last 10 years. Proven ability to work → less risk of unemployment in Canada.
④ Age 12 pts Max points ages 18–35. Younger workers have more years to contribute taxes and fill labour needs before retiring.
⑤ Job Offer 10 pts Arranged employment with a Canadian employer. Reduces unemployment risk; fills identified labour shortages immediately.
⑥ Adaptability 10 pts Previous study/work in Canada, relatives in Canada, spouse's education/language. More ties = easier settlement and success.
Q: What is the difference between a push and pull factor? Give one example of each for someone coming to Canada.
Push factor: A negative condition that forces or motivates someone to LEAVE their home country. Example: fleeing a civil war in Syria, or leaving a country with high unemployment and no economic opportunities.

Pull factor: A positive condition that ATTRACTS someone to Canada specifically. Example: Canada's reputation for safety, universal healthcare, job opportunities, or having family already living here. Both push and pull factors work together to drive someone's decision to migrate.
Q: What are the three main classes of immigrants Canada accepts? Which is the largest?
The three classes are: (1) Economic Class (~58%) — the largest group, selected through the points system based on skills and ability to work. (2) Family Class (~27%) — sponsored by a Canadian citizen or permanent resident (spouses, children, parents). (3) Refugee Class (~15%) — people fleeing war, persecution, or danger. The Economic Class is by far the largest.
Q: Why does Canada give the most points for "Language Skills" in the immigration points system?
Language skills earn the most points (28/100) because being able to communicate in English or French is essential for getting a job, integrating into society, and being self-sufficient in Canada. Immigrants who can't speak an official language struggle to find work and may require more government support. Strong language skills also help immigrants communicate with coworkers, use services, and participate in the community — making them more likely to succeed and contribute to the economy.

Where People Live
// population distribution · top 3 cities · rural vs urban · 4 settlement patterns
Population Distribution in Canada
  • Canada is the 2nd largest country by area but has a relatively small population (~40 million)
  • Population is very unevenly distributed — most people live in a narrow strip along the US border
  • About 90% of Canadians live within 200 km of the US border
  • Southern Ontario and Québec have the highest concentrations
  • Northern Canada (Nunavut, NWT, Yukon) = vast area but tiny population
  • Why? Milder climate, better agricultural land, more job opportunities, historical settlement routes
Top 3 Immigrant Destination Cities
① Toronto (GTA), Ontario

Canada's largest city and #1 destination — receives ~50% of Ontario's immigrants. One of the world's most multicultural cities. Major economic hub.

② Vancouver, British Columbia

2nd largest immigrant hub. Strong Pacific ties attract immigrants from China, Philippines, South Korea, and India. Mild climate is a pull factor.

③ Montréal, Québec

Québec selects its own immigrants (must speak French). Large Francophone immigrant community from North Africa, Caribbean, and France.

Rural vs. Urban Living
URBAN — Advantages
  • ✓ More job opportunities across many industries
  • ✓ Better access to hospitals, specialists, services
  • ✓ More schools, universities, cultural events
  • ✓ Public transit — less reliance on a car
  • ✓ Diverse communities, restaurants, entertainment
  • ✓ Immigrant services (ESL, settlement agencies)
URBAN — Disadvantages
  • ✗ Very high housing costs (Toronto, Vancouver)
  • ✗ Traffic congestion and long commutes
  • ✗ Higher cost of living overall
  • ✗ Pollution, noise, overcrowding
  • ✗ Less green space and nature access
RURAL — Advantages
  • ✓ Lower housing and land costs
  • ✓ More space, access to nature
  • ✓ Quieter, slower pace of life
  • ✓ Stronger sense of community
  • ✓ Less pollution and traffic
RURAL — Disadvantages
  • ✗ Fewer job opportunities
  • ✗ Less access to hospitals and specialists
  • ✗ Fewer schools, colleges, entertainment
  • ✗ Need a car — no public transit
  • ✗ Less diversity and fewer services for newcomers
4 Settlement Patterns
🌊
Linear / Along Water
Settlements along rivers, lakes, or coastlines. Early Canadian settlements followed waterways (St. Lawrence, Great Lakes). Water = transportation route + fresh water + fish.
🛣️
Linear / Along Roads
Settlements that develop along roads or railways. Homes/businesses line the main road. Common in Quebec's seigneurial system (long, narrow lots along roads).
🔵
Clustered / Nucleated
Homes and buildings grouped closely together around a central point (church, market square). Common in Quebec villages, European-style towns. Easier to share services and defense.
🌾
Dispersed / Scattered
Buildings spread widely across the landscape with no clear centre. Common in farming areas (Prairies) where large land grants spread farms far apart. Each farm is self-sufficient.
Q: Why do most immigrants to Canada settle in Toronto, Vancouver, or Montréal rather than rural areas?
Several pull factors draw immigrants to these cities: (1) Employment — large urban economies offer more jobs across many sectors. (2) Established ethnic communities — existing diaspora communities provide language support, cultural familiarity, and social networks. (3) Settlement services — ESL programs, immigrant support agencies, and social services are concentrated in cities. (4) Family connections — family-class immigrants follow sponsors who already live in cities. (5) Public transit — immigrants can get around without owning a car. Rural areas lack most of these supports.
Q: Describe one of the 4 settlement patterns and explain why people would have settled that way historically.
Example answer: Linear pattern along waterways — Early settlers in Canada built their communities along rivers and lakeshores because water was the most important resource. It provided fresh drinking water, a source of food (fish), and most importantly, a transportation route. Before roads and railways, rivers like the St. Lawrence were the highways of Canada — settlers needed to be close to them to trade goods, travel, and receive supplies.

Population Density
// definition · examples · Canada's density
What Is Population Density?

Population density is the number of people living in a given area, expressed as people per km².


Population Density = Total Population ÷ Area (km²)

It tells you how crowded or spread out a population is across the land.


  • High density: many people in a small area (cities)
  • Low density: few people spread across a large area (rural, northern Canada)
  • Canada's overall density ≈ 4 people/km² — one of the lowest in the world
  • But Canada's population is extremely uneven — most people are concentrated in the south
  • Toronto's density: ~4,400 people/km² vs. Nunavut: <0.1 people/km²
Why Population Density Varies in Canada
  • Climate: harsh winters in the North make settlement difficult; milder south is more habitable
  • Agricultural land: fertile farmland in southern Ontario and Prairies supports large populations
  • Resources: mines, oil sands, and forests attract workers to specific regions
  • Transportation: railways and highways developed in southern Canada; easier access = more settlement
  • History: original European settlements were in the south along waterways (St. Lawrence, Great Lakes)
  • Jobs: manufacturing, finance, and tech concentrated in cities → people follow jobs

Q: What is population density? Why is Canada's overall density low, even though cities like Toronto are very crowded?
Population density is the number of people per km² in an area. Canada's overall density is very low (~4 people/km²) because Canada is an enormous country (the 2nd largest in the world by area) with a relatively small population of ~40 million. However, most of those people are packed into a narrow strip along the US border in the south. Regions like Nunavut and the Northwest Territories cover millions of km² but have only a few thousand residents, which brings the national average way down. Toronto and Vancouver, by contrast, have very high local density because millions of people are living and working in a small urban area.
Q: Give two reasons why northern Canada has a very low population density.
(1) Harsh climate: The Arctic and subarctic North has extremely cold winters, permafrost, and short growing seasons, making it difficult to farm, build infrastructure, or live comfortably year-round. (2) Lack of economic opportunity: There are fewer industries, jobs, and services in northern communities. The remoteness makes it expensive to live there, and transportation is limited. Most Canadians stay in the south where jobs, healthcare, schools, and services are accessible.
