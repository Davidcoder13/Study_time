Immigrant Point System
// federal skilled worker · selection factors · pass mark
What Is the Point System?

Canada uses a points-based (Express Entry) system to select economic immigrants. Applicants are scored out of 100 points across six categories. The pass mark is typically 67 points to be eligible. The system is designed to admit immigrants who will contribute economically to Canada.

Selection Factor Max Points Key Details
① Education 25 pts Higher education = more points. PhD/Master's = 25, Bachelor's = 20, high school only = 5. Canadian credentials valued highly.
② Language Skills 28 pts Official languages (English & French). Tested via IELTS (English) or TEF (French). Higher fluency = more points. First language: up to 24 pts. Second: up to 4 pts.
③ Work Experience 15 pts Skilled work in NOC (National Occupational Classification) categories. More years = more points. Must be within last 10 years.
④ Age 12 pts Maximum points at ages 18–35. Points decrease after 35, reaching 0 at age 47+. Younger workers contribute longer to economy.
⑤ Arranged Employment 10 pts Having a valid, full-time job offer from a Canadian employer. Significantly boosts score.
⑥ Adaptability 10 pts Factors like: spouse's education or language, previous study/work in Canada, having relatives in Canada, arranged employment.
Pass Mark & How It Works
  • Minimum score to qualify: 67 out of 100
  • Total maximum points: 100 points
  • Economic class applicants use this system
  • Does not guarantee entry — candidates are ranked in a pool and top-scoring candidates receive Invitations to Apply (ITAs)
  • Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) score used in Express Entry draw
Why Canada Uses This System
  • Selects immigrants most likely to succeed economically
  • Addresses labour market gaps and skill shortages
  • Supports an aging population — younger workers pay taxes and support social programs
  • Language skills ensure better integration into Canadian society
  • Provides a fair, transparent, objective selection process
Q: Why does Canada give points for "Age" in the immigration system? Why are younger applicants favoured?
Younger immigrants have a longer working life ahead of them, meaning they will pay taxes and contribute to the economy for more years. Canada's aging population means there are more retirees drawing on government services (pensions, healthcare), so younger immigrants help offset this by paying into the system. Applicants aged 18–35 receive maximum points because they are at the start of their careers.
Q: An applicant has a university degree, intermediate English, 3 years experience, is 42 years old, and has no job offer. Estimate their approximate category scores and whether they'd likely pass.
Rough estimate: Education (university) ~20/25 · Language (intermediate) ~15/28 · Work Experience (3 yrs) ~11/15 · Age (42) ~6/12 · Employment (none) ~0/10 · Adaptability ~3/10 → Approx. 55/100 — this is below the 67-point pass mark. They would need to improve language scores, or obtain a job offer to have a realistic chance.

4 Types of Population Pyramids
① EXPANSIVE (Wide Base)

Wide base, narrow top. High birth rate, high death rate, low life expectancy. Young, fast-growing population. Found in: LEDCs (less economically developed countries) like Nigeria, Afghanistan.

② CONSTRICTIVE (Narrow Base)

Narrow base getting wider in middle. Low birth rate, aging population, slow or negative growth. Found in: MEDCs (more economically developed) like Germany, Japan, Canada.

③ STATIONARY (Rectangular/Column)

Almost equal-width bars throughout. Low birth rate, low death rate, long life expectancy. Stable/replacement-level population. Found in: some Northern/Western European countries.

④ BABY BOOM BULGE

A distinct bulge in a specific age cohort (middle of pyramid). Indicates a past baby boom — unusually high births in that era. Canada's baby boom was 1946–1965. Boomers are now in their 60s–80s.

Canada's Pyramid — Key Features
  • Constrictive / aging shape — narrow base, wide middle, wider top
  • Baby Boom bulge visible in ages 60–80 (born 1946–1965)
  • Low birth rate (~1.4 children per woman — below replacement of 2.1)
  • High life expectancy (~83 years)
  • Immigration is key to maintaining population growth
  • Dependency ratio increasing — more seniors per working-age person
What You Can Read from a Pyramid
  • Birth rate: width of the base (0–4 age group)
  • Death rate / life expectancy: how quickly bars narrow toward top
  • Gender imbalance: unequal left/right bars (e.g. war affects males)
  • Baby boom: a wider cohort in middle generations
  • Immigration: sudden bulge in young working-age groups (20–40)
  • Future growth: wide base = growing; narrow base = declining
Q: A population pyramid shows a very wide base (0–14) and a very narrow top (65+). What does this tell you about that country?
This is an expansive pyramid. The wide base shows a high birth rate. The narrow top shows a high death rate and low life expectancy — few people are surviving to old age. The population is young and growing rapidly. This is typical of a Less Economically Developed Country (LEDC) with limited access to healthcare and high fertility rates.

Key Definitions
// population terms · migration · demographic concepts
Core Population Terms
  • Birth Rate (CBR): number of live births per 1,000 people per year
  • Death Rate (CDR): number of deaths per 1,000 people per year
  • Natural Increase Rate (NIR): Birth Rate − Death Rate (expressed per 1,000 or as %)
  • Fertility Rate (TFR): average number of children a woman will have in her lifetime
  • Replacement Rate: TFR of ~2.1 children per woman needed to maintain population size
  • Life Expectancy: average number of years a person in a population is expected to live
  • Infant Mortality Rate: deaths of children under 1 year old per 1,000 live births
Migration Terms
  • Immigrant: a person who permanently moves to a new country (Canada = destination)
  • Emigrant: a person who permanently leaves their home country
  • Refugee: a person forced to flee their country due to war, persecution, or disaster
  • Push Factor: a negative condition driving someone to leave their home country (war, poverty, famine)
  • Pull Factor: a positive condition attracting someone to a destination country (jobs, safety, family)
  • Net Migration: immigration − emigration for a country in a given period
  • Brain Drain: emigration of skilled/educated people from a country
Population Distribution Terms
  • Population Density: number of people per km² in an area
  • Ecumene: the permanently inhabited portion of Earth's surface
  • Urban: a densely populated area (city or town)
  • Rural: low-density area outside cities; often agricultural
  • Urbanization: the process of populations moving from rural to urban areas
  • Census Metropolitan Area (CMA): urban core of 100,000+ people plus surrounding communities
  • Golden Horseshoe: densely populated region around Lake Ontario — Canada's largest urban cluster
Demographic Transition + Aging
  • Demographic Transition Model (DTM): model showing how birth/death rates change as a country develops (4 stages)
  • Dependency Ratio: ratio of non-working (young + elderly) to working-age (15–64) population
  • Baby Boom: significant increase in birth rates (Canada: 1946–1965 post-WWII)
  • Baby Bust: period following a boom when birth rates drop sharply
  • Aging Population: population with increasing proportion of elderly people (65+)
  • Population Pyramid: bar graph showing age and gender distribution of a population
Q: What is the difference between a "push factor" and a "pull factor"? Give an example of each for someone immigrating to Canada.
Push factor: a reason to LEAVE home country — e.g., fleeing a civil war in Syria, extreme poverty in Haiti, political persecution, or lack of employment.

Pull factor: a reason to CHOOSE Canada specifically — e.g., job opportunities, high standard of living, free healthcare, political stability, family already in Canada, or high quality of education. Both forces work together to drive migration decisions.

Immigrant Types
// economic · family · refugee · other classes
Canada's Immigration Classes — Overview
① ECONOMIC CLASS (~58%)

Selected using the points system. Includes Skilled Workers, Canadian Experience Class, and Business Immigrants. Largest category. Must demonstrate ability to work and contribute economically.

② FAMILY CLASS (~27%)

Sponsored by a Canadian citizen or permanent resident. Includes spouses, common-law partners, dependent children, parents, and grandparents. Sponsor must financially support the newcomer.

③ REFUGEE CLASS (~15%)

Protected persons fleeing persecution, war, or danger. Includes Government-Assisted Refugees (GARs) and Privately Sponsored Refugees. Canada-Born: claim status after arriving; Convention Refugees: recognized abroad first.

④ OTHER / HUMANITARIAN

Includes those admitted on humanitarian and compassionate grounds (H&C), or for other special circumstances. Also includes provincial nominees under Provincial Nominee Programs (PNPs).

Economic Sub-Categories
  • Federal Skilled Worker: uses the 6-factor points system; requires 67+ pts
  • Federal Skilled Trades: for carpenters, electricians, plumbers, etc.
  • Canadian Experience Class (CEC): for those who already have temporary work/study experience in Canada
  • Business Immigrants: investors, entrepreneurs, and self-employed persons who will create jobs or invest
  • Provincial Nominee Program (PNP): provinces nominate workers based on local labour needs
Refugee Sub-Categories
  • Convention Refugee: recognized by UNHCR outside Canada; meets 1951 UN Refugee Convention criteria (persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, social group)
  • Government-Assisted Refugee (GAR): selected abroad, fully supported by federal government for first year
  • Privately Sponsored Refugee: supported by a Canadian group/organization for first year
  • Asylum Seeker: claims refugee status after arriving in Canada; must be approved by Immigration and Refugee Board

Q: What is the difference between a "refugee" and an "economic immigrant"? Can a refugee ever use the points system?
An economic immigrant chooses to move to Canada voluntarily in search of better economic opportunities — they apply through the points system and are selected based on skills and contributions they'll make. A refugee is forced to flee their home country due to war, persecution, or danger — they are not selected through a points system; instead they are recognized based on a genuine need for protection. Refugees generally cannot use the points system — they access a separate humanitarian intake process.

Population Calculations
// formulas · worked examples · practice questions
① Natural Increase Rate (NIR)
NIR = Birth Rate − Death Rate
Result is per 1,000 people/year. To express as a % → divide by 10.
Example: Birth Rate = 18, Death Rate = 7 → NIR = 18 − 7 = 11 per 1,000 = 1.1%
② Population Growth Rate (includes migration)
Growth Rate = NIR + Net Migration Rate
Net Migration = Immigration rate − Emigration rate (per 1,000)
Example: NIR = 5, Net Migration = 8 → Growth Rate = 13 per 1,000 = 1.3%
③ Population Density
Population Density = Total Population ÷ Area (km²)
Example: Ontario population = 14,000,000 / Area = 1,076,395 km² → Density ≈ 13 people/km²
Note: Ontario's density is uneven — most people are in the southern Golden Horseshoe.
④ Doubling Time (Rule of 70)
Doubling Time = 70 ÷ Growth Rate (%)
Example: A country with 2% growth rate → 70 ÷ 2 = 35 years to double its population.
Canada's growth rate (~1%) → doubles in ~70 years.
⑤ Dependency Ratio
Dependency Ratio = (Population 0–14 + Population 65+) ÷ Population 15–64 × 100
Shows how many dependents (children + seniors) exist per 100 working-age people.
Example: 25 youth + 20 seniors per 55 working adults → (45 ÷ 55) × 100 = 82 dependents per 100 workers.
Higher = more strain on working-age population (taxes, social services).
⑥ Future Population (simple growth)
Future Pop = Current Pop × (1 + Growth Rate)^Years
Example: Current pop = 40 million, Growth = 1% → After 10 years: 40,000,000 × (1.01)^10 ≈ 44.2 million
Q: A country has a birth rate of 22/1,000 and death rate of 6/1,000. Its immigration rate is 5/1,000 and emigration rate is 2/1,000. What is: (a) the NIR, (b) the Net Migration, and (c) the total growth rate?
(a) NIR = Birth Rate − Death Rate = 22 − 6 = 16 per 1,000 (1.6%)

(b) Net Migration = Immigration − Emigration = 5 − 2 = 3 per 1,000

(c) Total Growth Rate = NIR + Net Migration = 16 + 3 = 19 per 1,000 (1.9%)
Q: A country has a growth rate of 3.5%. Use the Rule of 70 to find its doubling time. Is this fast or slow?
Doubling Time = 70 ÷ 3.5 = 20 years. This is very fast — the population would double in just two decades. This is typical of high-growth developing countries and creates challenges for food, housing, jobs, schools, and healthcare infrastructure.

Settlement Trends
// where immigrants settle · urbanization · golden horseshoe · barriers
Where Do Immigrants Settle in Canada?
ONTARIO (~50%)

Receives the most immigrants. GTA (Greater Toronto Area) is the #1 destination. Toronto is one of the most diverse cities in the world.

BC (~17%)

Vancouver is the 2nd largest immigrant destination. Strong ties to Asia-Pacific. Warm climate and Pacific location are pull factors.

QUÉBEC (~17%)

Montréal is a major destination. Québec selects its own economic immigrants (must be French-speaking). Large Francophone immigrant community.

PRAIRIES (~12%)

Calgary and Edmonton growing fast. PNP programs attract skilled workers needed for oil & gas, agriculture. More affordable than TO/Vancouver.

Why Immigrants Choose Major Cities
  • Employment: more job opportunities in diverse urban economies
  • Ethnic communities: existing communities provide social support, language, cultural services
  • Services: better access to settlement agencies, ESL programs, healthcare, schools
  • Transportation: urban transit; less need for a car
  • Family reunification: sponsors often already living in cities
  • Education: universities, colleges, and schools concentrated in cities
The Golden Horseshoe
  • Densely populated urban region wrapping around the western end of Lake Ontario
  • Includes: Toronto, Mississauga, Brampton, Hamilton, Oshawa, Burlington, Niagara Falls
  • Home to over 9+ million people — Canada's most densely populated region
  • Receives the majority of Ontario's immigrants
  • Major economic hub: finance, manufacturing, tech, film, trade through Port of Hamilton
  • Urban sprawl = loss of agricultural land (Greenbelt meant to protect farmland)
Challenges Immigrants Face
  • Foreign credential recognition: degrees and professional qualifications not always accepted
  • Language barriers: English/French proficiency takes time; limits employment access
  • Housing costs: Toronto and Vancouver have extremely high real estate prices
  • Discrimination: visible minorities may face systemic barriers in hiring
  • Social isolation: leaving family/community behind; cultural adjustment
  • "Canadian experience" catch-22: employers want experience, but immigrants can't get experience without a job
Rural Settlement Initiatives
  • Government trying to direct more immigrants to rural and smaller communities to fill labour shortages
  • Provincial Nominee Programs (PNPs) allow provinces to target specific skills needed locally (e.g. healthcare in rural ON)
  • Rural and Northern Immigration Pilot: federal program offering PR to those who settle in participating smaller communities
  • Challenge: smaller communities have fewer services, ethnic communities, and employment opportunities
  • Many immigrants initially settle rurally but migrate to cities within 5 years
Historical Change in Source Countries
Before 1960s — European Dominated

Majority from UK, Italy, Germany, Ukraine, Poland. Largely white, Christian. Immigration driven by post-WWII labour demand.

1967 — Points System Introduced

Removed racial discrimination from selection. Opened immigration to all countries equally. Shift toward merit-based system.

1970s–1990s — Asia/Caribbean Surge

Hong Kong Chinese (pre-1997 handover), South Asian, Caribbean, Filipino immigrants grew rapidly.

2000s–Present — Highly Diverse

Top source countries now: India, China, Philippines, Nigeria, Pakistan, Syria. Asia source of most economic immigrants. Greater visible minority representation.

Q: Why do most immigrants to Canada settle in Toronto, Vancouver, and Montréal rather than in rural areas?
Several pull factors draw immigrants to major cities: (1) Employment — diverse urban economies offer more job opportunities across many sectors. (2) Established ethnic communities — existing diaspora communities provide language support, cultural familiarity, and social networks. (3) Settlement services — ESL programs, immigrant support agencies, and social services are concentrated in cities. (4) Family connections — family class immigrants follow sponsors who already live in cities. (5) Transit and infrastructure — urban public transit reduces need to own a car. Rural areas lack many of these supports.
